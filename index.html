Sliding window

  • Only the last 26 non-troll kerkis count (roughly half a year).
  • Of those, only your best 18 results are summed — the rest are dropped.
  • Troll kerkis (#10, #20, #30) are excluded entirely.

How it works

  • 1st Winner is the first player to clinch — they beat the full lobby. After they leave, the field is weaker, so 2nd–5th earn fewer points.
  • The best-of system means missing a few kerkis doesn't destroy your ranking, but you can't coast on old results forever.
  • Theoretical max: 18 × 200 = 3600 — you'd need to win 1st in every single kerki.

Tiers

  • Master — 800+ points
  • Gold — 200+ points

Convergent Skill Rating

A Glicko-inspired skill estimate for the Kerki Comp. Each result pulls your rating toward a performance value based on your placement.

How it works

  • Every player starts at 1500 (unproven baseline).
  • Each kerki result pulls your rating toward a performance value — winning 1st pulls toward 2100, finalist toward 1700, etc.
  • Early results cause big jumps (high uncertainty). After several kerkis, your rating stabilizes and moves slowly.
  • The rating converges to your average performance level over time.

Decay & uncertainty

  • Inactivity decay — miss a kerki and your rating decays 2% toward 1500. You can't sit on a high rating without playing.
  • Surprise widening — if your result is far from your current rating (e.g. a 1700-rated player wins 1st), the system becomes less certain and allows bigger adjustments. This prevents players from getting stuck.
  • ±σ shows uncertainty — high σ means the system hasn't seen enough data to be confident. Low σ means the rating is settled.

Scale

The scale is calibrated to match COTD ELO — a 1800 Kerki skill rating roughly corresponds to a 1800 COTD ELO player. This was validated by comparing Kerki ratings against known COTD ratings across 48 players.

Limitations

  • No lobby context — winning against 30 players gives the same rating as winning against 7. Full lobby data (from Kerki 33+) will improve accuracy.
  • Players with few appearances have high uncertainty — their rating may not reflect true skill yet.
